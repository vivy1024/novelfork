# Chapter 6: Where the Marks Were Moved

The hall steps still held the day’s heat when Rowan came back down them.

People had not gone home. Brackenside lived in shifts and arguments. Cart wheels knocked over cracked stone. Someone nearby roasted cave eel over a brazier, the oily smell fighting with lime dust and the medicinal tang drifting out from the healing ward. Half the square was talking about the fresh subsidence. The other half was pretending not to.

Lio waited below with his hands in his pockets and his shoulders bunched against trouble. Sena stood a few paces away, chin up, face tired and hard.

“Well?” Lio asked.

“Temporary win,” Rowan said, adjusting the sling biting under his neck.

“That sounds like losing with paperwork,” Sena said.

“It usually is.”

Lio gave a short laugh. “They keeping the file open?”

“For Benn and Derren, yes.”

Sena let out a breath. “And Pike?”

“Denied observer rights on the deposition.” Rowan glanced toward the street, where crews were already clustering to trade guesses as if that made a thing safer. “He’ll try another door.”

“He always does,” Lio said.

A runner hurried past with a stamped tube in one hand and a strip of yellow hazard cloth in the other. If South Third had gone from rumor to posted cloth, the outer cordon would go up before the hour turned.

Rowan started down the lane.

Lio kept pace. “You’re not going home.”

“No.”

Sena fell in on Rowan’s other side. “Your father will love that.”

“He can complain after I’m done.”

“He’ll complain during too,” Lio said. “That’s his craft.”

Rowan almost smiled. “Then he can practice.”

They cut through the lower market, where ore buyers were already working the fear. Every fresh collapse made prices twitch. Purified cores, salvageable fittings, alchemical fungus vats, monster materials, old relay glass, rustless fasteners pulled from dead sections—Brackenside survived on what came up from dungeons and ruins, and on what could be refined, salvaged, grown, or stripped from danger. The guilds survived by controlling entry points, licensing, maps, and legal salvage rights around it all.

At the next corner, Sena slowed.

“I’m not welcome at a cordon,” she said. “Too many people saw me point at men with badges.”

“That’s because they earned it,” Lio muttered.

Rowan looked at her. “Then don’t go to the line. Go to Benn’s sister.”

Sena frowned. “Why?”

“If the file’s still open, someone will ask whether the family wants a formal search extension if the district shifts to hazard closure. They’ll word it politely. It means costs, waivers, and blame. Make sure she hears it plain before somebody sells her panic.”

Sena considered that, then nodded. “You’re getting less bad at this.”

“I’ll treasure the praise.”

“Don’t. I may want it back.” She stepped away, then looked over her shoulder. “If you find anything true, not guild true, send for me.”

Rowan dipped his chin. She left at once.

Lio watched her go. “She walks like she’s late for a fight.”

“She usually is.”

They turned south.

The nearer they got, the thinner the traffic became and the louder everything sounded. Hammering from a brace shop. The ring of chain against iron hooks. A mule snorting in a side yard. Then the cordon came into view across South Third’s feeder lane: hazard posts, yellow cloth, two watchmen, three guild hands in dust capes, and a survey tripod beside a stack of slate markers.

Not a full closure. Yet.

“Good,” Rowan said.

“That?” Lio said. “Your standards are warped.”

“Full closure means no talking.”

“Ah. We’ve upgraded to terrible.”

One of the guild hands noticed them and straightened. Rowan knew him by face if not by name: narrow cheeks, scar at the lip, one of Hadrik’s outer-circle survey men.

“No access,” the man said before Rowan could speak.

“I didn’t ask for any.”

“That’s usually the next line.”

“Then you’ve robbed me of my art.”

The man didn’t smile. “This district is under review. Authorized crews only.”

Rowan stepped up to the hazard cloth without touching it. “I want the posted facts. Subsidence line, marker report, whether there was sound.”

“Why?”

“Because two men are still missing in there.”

“Missing-party file is a hall matter.”

“And hazard assessment isn’t?”

Another voice answered from beside the tripod. “It is, but posted facts don’t belong to you just because you ask in a level tone.”

The speaker was older, broad through the middle, with stone dust caught in his beard and a brass tally clip at his belt. Workman neat. A district measurer, maybe.

Rowan turned. “Then who do they belong to?”

“Anyone with standing to read them.” The older man looked him over. “You the Vale boy from the hearing?”

“Depends who’s asking.”

“Darm Pell. District measure office.” He jerked his chin at the cloth line. “You made my evening longer.”

“That makes two of us.”

Pell pointed with a charcoal stub to a slate board propped near the tripod. “Read.”

The notes were clipped and ugly. Fresh sink along the south shoulder. Two search markers displaced. One broken. Disturbance direction uncertain due to crumble. Interior drag or airflow not ruled out. No confirmed voice response at third-bell test. Reentry delayed pending stability check.

Rowan read the middle line twice.

“Broken how?” he asked.

Pell shrugged. “Not by weather.”

“Not by a settling crack either,” Rowan said.

“No.”

Lio looked between them. “For those of us not married to slate, why does that matter?”

“Marker posts are set to show direction,” Rowan said. “If stone falls from above, you can usually read it. If the scrape is low and inward, someone struck it from below or brushed past it moving out.”

“Or something,” Lio said.

Pell’s gaze sharpened. “You’ve seen displaced search markers before.”

“Enough.”

Pell tapped the line again. “No voice response.”

“Third bell through fresh rubble means little,” Rowan said. “Sound catches. Lies.”

Pell studied him. “Every second man with a grievance has turned expert this week.”

“Every second man with a stamp is pretending uncertainty means ownership,” Rowan said.

The younger survey hand stiffened. “Watch your tone.”

“No,” Pell said without looking at him. “Let him spend it.”

Rowan let the silence sit, then asked, “If this is enough to justify an outer probe, who gets sent?”

Pell narrowed his eyes. “Now we reach the real question.”

“Part of it.”

“Authorized review crew,” Pell said at last. “Measure office, guild safety men, two licensed delvers if the hall signs off.”

“And Pike petitions for one of the licenses,” Rowan said.

The younger hand gave a tiny guilty glance away. Lio swore under his breath.

Pell looked annoyed rather than surprised. “Petitions are not assignments.”

“No. They’re just pressure written in a nicer hand.”

Pell exhaled. “True.”

Rowan pressed on. “What about the old maintenance maps for South Third?”

Pell’s head came up at once. “Why?”

“For route comparison. If a support line failed where older cuts intersect, that matters.”

“To whom?”

“To anyone deciding whether the district shifted along known voids.”

Pell looked unimpressed. “You want maps, go through registry.”

“They’ll tell me to wait for deposition review.”

“Probably.”

“And by then the useful parts vanish into copied abstracts.”

The younger survey hand laughed once. “Scholar’s discovered the world.”

“I discovered it when I saw what they left off the first map I was given,” Rowan said.

That took the smile off him.

Pell rubbed at his beard. “South Third maintenance records aren’t public pull.”

“Because?”

“Because old work routes become present theft routes the moment a fool spreads them.”

That was true enough. Old service passages, forgotten sump runs, abandoned vent cuts—those were worth coin, leverage, and lives.

“So you do have them,” Rowan said.

“I didn’t say that.”

“You didn’t have to.”

Pell grunted. “You are very young to be this tiring.”

“Bad food. Harsh upbringing.”

Lio coughed into his fist.

Pell looked at the sling again. “And despite that, you came here to ask for records nobody sensible hands an unlicensed salvage runner standing under active review.”

“When you say it like that, it sounds difficult.”

“It is.”

“Good. Easy things tend to be worthless.”

The older man stared at him, then gestured toward a side shed built against the district wall. “Walk with me.”

The younger survey hand protested at once. “Master Pell—”

“If I planned to hand him the district keys, Sern, I’d do it where you could admire the mistake. Hold the line.”

Sern didn’t like it. Rowan noticed that.

Pell led them to the shed’s shaded side, out of the lane’s direct view but not out of earshot of the cordon. Private enough for a warning, public enough to deny conspiracy.

“Your hearing has everyone stirred up,” Pell said. “Hadrik wants this review neat. Pike wants it useful. The hall wants it slow enough to seem careful and quick enough to look humane. You understand me?”

“Yes.”

“Do you? Because asking after old maintenance cuts the same hour a disturbed marker report goes public is a fine way to get your shadow counted.”

“It already is.”

“Counted harder.”

Pell took a folded waxed strip from his pocket. “Before I decide whether you’re worth even this much trouble, tell me why you care about maintenance records more than about whether those men are alive.”

Lio looked at Rowan sharply. The question had teeth because it was unfair and because it wasn’t.

“Because if they’re alive, the way to them matters more than a prayer shouted into rubble,” Rowan said. “And if they’re dead, I still want to know why a patched barrier sat in a route no current chart marked properly.”

Pell’s gaze stayed hard. “Patched barrier.”

“So you’ve heard that too.”

“I hear many things.”

“Then hear one more. Somebody worked stone in there after the old line was abandoned, and not recently. Different bond, older hand, cleaner seam under dust. That wall didn’t happen by cave mood.”

Lio frowned at him. “You never said older.”

“I said enough for the hearing.”

“That mean not enough for me?”

“It meant not in front of a room that would sell the answer.”

Lio muttered, “I hate when your caution makes sense.”

Pell turned the waxed strip over once in his fingers. “Suppose I believed you. Suppose there is older work tied into South Third’s dead cuts. Why would I help you look?”

“Because if your office misses a legacy void or sealed service line and sends a neat review crew into bad stone, that goes on your count.”

Pell’s jaw shifted.

“And because whoever disturbed those markers came from somewhere.”

“Or was pushed,” Lio said quietly.

That settled between them.

Pell finally held out the strip. “Not a map. Shelf reference. Old district maintenance abstracts. Incomplete. Archive annex, lower record room. If the clerk on duty is Nessa, say I sent you. If it’s Varn, leave. He reports every curious breath to the guild side.”

Rowan took the strip.

“Why?” he asked.

“Because I dislike neat disasters. And because I buried a nephew after a review crew trusted a copied summary over an original brace log.” Pell looked back toward the cordon. “Also because Sern has been too interested in who gets into South Third.”

“You think he’s feeding Pike?” Rowan asked.

“I think young men with debts talk too much to men who promise faster ladders.”

Lio muttered, “That’s a yes in workman.”

Pell ignored him. “Listen carefully. You never got this from me. You were never invited to ask after maintenance logs. If anyone asks, you came here for the posted facts, got them, and left disappointed.”

“There’s the part where he lies to authority again,” Lio said.

“Only because authority keeps demanding payment for truth.”

Pell gave Rowan a level stare. “If I find you trying to breach the cordon or stir a freelance search, I’ll put you in a holding room myself. Missing men don’t improve under dead rescuers.”

“I know.”

Pell seemed to accept that. “Go.”

Only once they’d turned two corners away did Lio speak.

“You hid that from me.”

“Yes.”

“Don’t like that.”

“I know.”

“That wall. The older workmanship.” Lio kept his voice low. “You thought if you said it aloud in the hearing, they’d tear the route open and lock you out.”

“Yes.”

“And now you think old records might tell you who patched it.”

“Maybe.”

Lio walked in silence for a few steps, then said, “You’re not wrong. You’re still irritating, but you’re not wrong. If Pike hears ‘older hidden structure,’ he’ll have half the district swearing salvage priority before dawn.”

“He already suspects enough.”

“Suspecting and proving aren’t the same coin.”

“No.”

The archive annex occupied the backside of an old tally house wedged between the measure office stores and a relay shed full of retired brass tubes. It smelled of dust, lamp oil, and damp paper.

A bell over the door clicked as they entered.

Behind a waist-high counter sat a woman with iron-gray hair pinned through with two quills. She was copying index marks into a ledger without looking up.

“Closed in twenty minutes,” she said. “Requests in writing if it’s guild, in duplicate if it’s hall, and if it’s personal grievance you may take one of the stools and improve your character.”

Lio leaned toward Rowan. “I like her.”

Rowan set the waxed strip on the counter. “Darm Pell said to ask for lower-room maintenance abstracts. South Third district.”

That brought her eyes up.

“Nessa,” she said.

“Good.”

“Not necessarily.” She checked the thumb-mark in the wax and looked from Rowan to his sling to Lio’s expression. “You’re the hearing boy.”

“I’ve been called worse.”

“South Third maintenance abstracts are restricted pulls.”

“So I’ve learned.”

“And yet here you are.”

“Also learned.”

Lio sighed. “He does this until doors open or throw bricks. Usually doors.”

Nessa considered them, then rose carefully. “You may view index abstracts only. No copies. No full route folios. No loose handling. If I think either of you is memorizing beyond your station, I’ll sneeze, and three very bored clerks will appear with civic enthusiasm.”

Lio blinked. “You have dedicated anti-reading staff?”

“I have clerks. The rest is attitude.”

She led them down a narrow stair into a lower room where the air turned cooler and smelled faintly of mildew and paste glue. Shelves lined the walls, packed with stitched bundles, slate tubes, and bound abstracts tagged by district age. Somewhere water dripped with maddening patience.

Nessa set a shallow tray on a reading stand and brought three thin stitched packets.

“South Third,” she said. “Maintenance summary. Brace revisions. Closure annotations. Abstracted from originals after the Fire Ledger reforms, so if something vanished before then, you will enjoy not knowing it. Twenty minutes.”

Rowan opened the first packet.

The script was compact and selective. Main braces. Drain channels. Vent complaints. Collapse notes. Partial abandonments. Then, midway through a closure annotation twenty-three years old, a line caught him.

Secondary bell conduit sealed at old service seam by order of district master. Masonry patch accepted pending later reinforcement.

He felt a small cold movement under his ribs.

“Lio.”

“What?”

“Here.”

Lio leaned in. “Bell conduit?”

“Secondary.”

“That means what?”

“Not a main signal line. Auxiliary. Maybe maintenance call, maybe old warning route.” Rowan scanned downward. “Wait.”

Below that sat a cramped note added in another hand years later.

Later reinforcement deferred. Access notation transferred to vault index.

“Vault,” Lio said quietly.

“Could mean record vault.”

“You don’t sound convinced.”

“I’m not.”

He turned pages. Another abstract. Another closure note. Then a brace revision report mentioning dead resonance in south wall cavities after conduit seal. Report filed, no action due to planned district cutback.

Dead resonance.

A bell memory pulsed at the back of Rowan’s mind—the way stone had seemed to hold a note too long near that hidden wall.

Lio tapped a margin mark. “This symbol.”

“Transfer mark.”

“To where?”

“That’s the problem.”

Footsteps sounded at the stair.

Nessa’s voice floated down. “You have six minutes, and someone unpleasant is upstairs asking whether I’ve seen two unauthorized men sniffing at district records.”

Lio went still. “That sounds aimed.”

“It often is,” Nessa called back.

Rowan closed the top packet halfway, thinking fast.

“Can they come down?” he asked.

“They can try,” Nessa said. “Whether they do depends on how badly they want me in their next complaint file.”

Rowan slid the relevant packet under the other two, not stealing it, just burying the exact page from quick view.

Nessa descended enough to show one severe eye. “If you’ve found something worth your present expression, boy, either speak quickly or learn to look more foolish.”

Rowan pointed at the closure annotation. “This transfer mark. Vault index. Where would pre-reform district vault references have gone?”

Nessa’s eye sharpened.

“That is not a question you should ask while being looked for.”

“Someone upstairs from guild?”

“Worse. Polite.”

Which meant hall or someone hired to sound like it.

Lio swore softly. “Pike?”

“Maybe. Maybe a clerk with ambition.” Nessa came down the last steps, snatched the top packet aside, read the line Rowan indicated, and went very still. “Ah.”

That one small sound said she knew more than she wanted to.

“Where?” Rowan asked.

Nessa looked toward the ceiling, then back to the page.

“There is no public vault index for pre-reform district transfers,” she said.

Lio made an exasperated noise. “Of course there isn’t.”

Nessa ignored him. “There is, however, a retired shelf of orphaned reference slates that should have been culled and never were because nobody likes old basements enough to finish the work. You did not hear that from me.”

“But,” she added, voice flat as cut tin, “if the person upstairs is who I think it is, and if they’re asking after you now, then whatever moved those markers has already done one useful thing.”

Rowan felt the room narrow.

“What useful thing?”

Nessa looked at the transfer note, then at him.

“It convinced someone aboveground to start searching records before you did.”

From upstairs came the scrape of the front door opening wider, then a man’s voice—calm, educated, and entirely too sure of itself.

“Archivist Nessa? Hall review authority. I’m told Rowan Vale may be on the premises.”

Lio’s hand tightened on the edge of the reading stand.

Rowan folded the packet shut.

If the hall was searching records tonight, then the race had already started.
