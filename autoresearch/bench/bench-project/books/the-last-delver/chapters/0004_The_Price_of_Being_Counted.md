# Chapter 4: The Price of Being Counted

Rowan slept hard and badly.

He woke with grit in his teeth, one shoulder stiff where stone had clipped him, and the smell of boiled linen thick in his nose. The healing ward was really a long canvas shelter against the guild annex, open at both ends, cots packed close enough to hear strangers breathe. Someone nearby coughed wetly. Somewhere beyond, metal rang from the sorting yards, steady as a hammer.

Morning, then.

Rowan rolled onto his side and regretted it.

“Good,” said a voice two cots over. “You look miserable. Means you’re alive.”

Lio sat up with his bandaged arm in a sling, pale beneath dust that washing hadn’t fully removed. His face was narrow and alert even half-asleep.

“You always this pleasant?” Rowan asked.

“Only for heroes.”

“Try that again and I’ll walk out.”

“You were already going to.”

True enough.

Rowan sat up. At the far end of the shelter, Toma slept with one leg braced and bound. Jeren lay awake but still, staring upward as if expecting rock to come through canvas. Sena was arguing in a low hiss with a medic over whether she could leave. Mett was gone.

“They let folk walk?” Rowan asked.

“If they think you can stand without falling on their floor.” Lio swung his feet down. “They also let clerks in before breakfast, if that tells you what kind of charity this is.”

“Who?”

“Two from registry. One from claims. One from Pike Holdings pretending not to be from Pike Holdings.”

“Did they get statements?”

“The fast ones. They were fishing.”

“For the route.”

“For anything that turns into the route once it’s written by someone with a seal.”

Rowan stood, waited for the black spots to pass, and pulled on his mostly clean shirt. One bootlace had snapped; he tied it off with cord from an old bandage.

Lio watched. “You planning to stride in and win with charm?”

“I’m planning to know the price before someone tells me I can’t afford it.”

“There. Almost wisdom.”

They left the shelter together. Morning air carried coal smoke, damp earth, and the sour bite of alchemical vats downslope. Beyond the annex, Brackenside climbed in cramped layers of stone, scaffold braces, and ore lifts. Roofs were patched with scavenged sheet metal, walls wore old stains and newer guild notices. The town lived on what came from below: dungeon ore, salvage, relic-tech, alchemical fungus, monster materials, purified cores. Everybody ate because somebody went under.

And the guild made sure nobody went below cleanly. Guilds controlled entry points, licensing, maps, and legal salvage rights.

The side yard by the annex was already crowded with workers waiting for treatment slips, pay vouchers, release stamps, or mercy on paper. A woman argued with a clerk over damaged salvage tags. Two watchmen stood by the gate.

Lio angled them away from the main queue. “Provisional screen runs through the hall, but hall doesn’t mean free. There’s the filing fee. If you don’t have a sponsor in good standing, there’s a bond note. If you pass, you go under probation. Any infraction and they yank the paper and keep the money.”

“How much?”

Lio named a number.

Rowan stopped. “You’re joking.”

“I’m thin, not funny.”

The amount was absurd for a runner with debt already hanging over his household.

“There’s no cheaper route?” Rowan asked.

“There’s the indenture route.”

“No.”

“Thought so.”

They walked past stacked fungus crates sweating earthy damp through their slats.

“Bond note,” Rowan said quietly. “Who signs one?”

“A licensed delver. Team lead. Hall factor. Sometimes a contractor.”

“Anyone likely to do that for me?”

Lio gave him a dry look. “Do you know many licensed delvers eager to take legal risk for an unranked salvage runner who embarrassed a claims man in public?”

“Then why say it like it’s an option?”

“Because rare isn’t impossible.”

Rowan glanced at him.

Lio lifted his good hand. “Not me. I don’t have the paper for it. I’m provisional myself, just further along than you thought.”

“Why not advertise it?”

“Because in Brackenside, any new line on your record becomes a handle someone uses.”

They cut behind two sheds into a quieter lane. Someone had dumped wash water against the wall; the stones smelled faintly of lye.

Lio leaned one shoulder on the brick. “Practical bad, then. The screen isn’t one test. It’s three. Rules and route law first. Basic field triage second. Controlled delve exercise third.”

Rowan looked at him.

“Restricted access exercise,” Lio amended.

“Much better.”

“Thought so. You hate their words almost as much as I do.”

“Rules I can learn,” Rowan said. “Triage I can manage if they mean bandages and splints.”

“They do. Plus don’t-panic questions.”

“And the last?”

“They put candidates into a marked section. Hazards, bad footing, timed decisions, salvage judgment. It’s meant to prove you won’t die stupid and won’t steal from the hall blind.”

“Can it be gamed?”

“Everything can be gamed.”

Footsteps sounded at the lane mouth.

A man in a dark coat came in with a ledger board under one arm, too clean for yard work and too plain for a guild officer. He was young, broad-shouldered, with close-cut hair and an old scalp scar. A runner Rowan recognized from registry trailed behind him.

The man smiled without warmth. “Vale. Good. Saves me a search.”

Lio went still. Rowan didn’t. “Who’s asking?”

“Call me Hadrik. I’m collecting clarifications for parties affected by the South Third failure.”

“Funny way to say scavenging statements.”

Hadrik’s smile sharpened. “And you’d be Lio Marr. You’ve been difficult.”

“I have that effect on men with boards.”

The runner snorted and hid it.

Hadrik ignored him. “You two were among the last confirmed out from the collapse pocket. There are families waiting on word. Liability questions. Also the matter of unregistered movement through restricted workings.”

Concern, cost, and threat in one coat.

Rowan said, “If this is official, get an official tabard.”

“It’s practical. Official channels take time.”

“Then take time.”

Hadrik shifted his attention to Rowan. “You’re in no position to be precious. Two men remain unaccounted for. Benn Corl and Derren Holt. If you have information—”

“I gave the order to withdraw,” Rowan said. “Write that down clean.”

The runner’s stylus scratched.

Lio cut in. “Write the conditions too. Secondary collapse risk. Injured civilians. No licensed lead present.”

Hadrik didn’t look at him. “Did you hear either missing man after your party entered the old workings?”

“No,” Rowan said.

“Did you see signs they had moved through the false wall passage before you?”

“No.”

“Did you identify any alternate shafts, maintained rails, sealed doors, service markers, or relic fixtures beyond ordinary abandoned cut?”

Too specific.

Rowan felt Lio’s warning in the silence beside him. Don’t.

He shrugged. “Mostly dark stone and people trying not to die. If there were markers, I was busy bleeding.”

Hadrik watched him. “Several others were less distracted.”

“Then ask them.”

“I have.”

So they were comparing stories already.

Hadrik stepped closer, smelling faintly of lamp oil and expensive soap. “If the corridor you used passes through guild-retained infrastructure, concealing it becomes obstruction. Obstruction affects qualification review. So does withholding relevant evidence in a missing-persons inquiry.”

Rowan held his gaze. “Then file an inquiry through someone with a seal.”

Hadrik’s jaw tightened. “You think stubbornness is leverage.”

“No. Paper is leverage. Which is why you’re here before breakfast.”

The runner’s stylus stopped.

Lio said lightly, “You’re also wasting the window where families are easier to frighten than angry.”

Hadrik rounded on him. “You want anger, Marr? Keep prodding.”

“Better than kneeling.”

The lane went still.

At last Hadrik checked his temper and looked back to Rowan. “There’ll be a formal board this afternoon. Attendance expected. If you fail to appear, your retained notice may be reviewed unfavorably.”

Rowan frowned. “My what?”

“Ask at the hall.” Hadrik smiled again, then turned and left with his runner.

Lio watched him go. “I dislike him.”

“That makes two of us.”

“Three, if we count the runner. He looked tired.”

They set off again, faster.

“You knew there might be a board?” Rowan asked.

“A review, yes. This soon? No.”

“Retained notice.”

“Likely means the Ledger marked your action, and the hall hasn’t decided whether to validate or bury it. Which is exactly why you don’t feed Hadrik a map with your mouth.”

Board this afternoon. Qualification fee. Bond note. Missing men. Witnesses being separated. If Pike’s side got enough statements first, they could turn the old corridor into guild-retained property before Rowan had standing to contest anything.

“Who from our group can still talk?” he asked.

“Sena. Maybe Jeren if you don’t mind waiting between every third word. Toma’s doped enough to thank blankets. Mett vanished.”

“Convenient.”

“Mett lives by being hard to find. That’s not proof of guilt.”

“No. Just proof of Mett.”

They crossed into older streets where guild stone gave way to brick and patched timber. Small shops crowded shoulder to shoulder: boot menders, fungus brokers, a woman selling hot eel pies beneath a striped awning. The smell made Rowan’s stomach knot.

Lio noticed. “Eat.”

“With what?”

“I’ve got enough for pies or dignity. Pick one.”

“Keep your coin.”

“Then pick stubborn starvation.”

Rowan bought a pie. The filling was mostly onion and pepper with enough eel to justify the lie, but it was hot and good.

They ate on a covered stair overlooking the lower debt quarter. Below lay rows of narrow houses where contract workers lived stacked tight when work was good and tighter when it wasn’t. Laundry lines crossed alleys like truce flags. Somewhere a child laughed. Somewhere someone was already shouting about money.

Rowan thought of his father.

Elias would know by now Rowan was alive. Relief would last only until the next knock from a collector.

“You’re thinking about the money,” Lio said.

“I’m thinking about all of it.”

“Dangerous habit.”

Rowan looked toward the lane leading to his block. “If I can’t pay the filing fee, none of the rest matters.”

“Maybe.”

“That means you have a bad idea.”

“A creative one.”

“Worse.”

“There are people who’ll front the fee if they smell profit.”

“Indenture with cleaner handwriting.”

“Not always. Sometimes it’s a private bond. Short term. Ugly interest. Personal risk instead of labor lock.”

“That’s not better.”

“No. Just different.”

After a moment Lio added, “There’s another route. If enough witnesses attest that you led an emergency recovery with claim-relevant judgment, the hall can waive part of the practical screen and reduce the bond burden.”

“That sounded almost hopeful.”

“You shouldn’t trust it. It still needs signatures, and signatures come from people who may be leaned on.”

“Sena.”

“Maybe.”

“Jeren.”

“If he thinks speaking won’t cost his job.”

Rowan crumpled the pie paper. “And the families of Benn and Derren will hear I walked out without them.”

Lio didn’t soften it. “Yes.”

Good. Better the truth than excuses.

“Then I tell them myself before someone else does,” Rowan said.

Lio stared. “That is either honorable or stupid.”

“Usually both.”

“Fine. We start with Sena. Then Jeren if the medics let us in. Then the families, if you’re still set on walking into that fire.”

“I am.”

They found Sena in the annex yard arguing with a registrar over release time and lost tools. One shoulder was wrapped, jaw hard, dark hair tied back with cloth. The registrar retreated the moment she saw Rowan and Lio waiting.

Sena turned on them. “If this is about thanking you, don’t. I hate scenes before noon.”

“It’s not,” Rowan said.

“Good.”

Lio leaned on a fence post. “He needs a witness willing to say he kept us alive and didn’t invent the old route after the fact.”

Sena’s expression shut. “Ah.”

Rowan said, “I’m also going to speak to Benn’s people. And Derren’s.”

“You really are stupid,” she said.

“Been told.”

She looked toward the sorting yard where haulers loaded carts with fungus crates and tagged salvage sacks. “I’ll tell the truth. Not the polished kind. You found the wall. You pushed us through. You made the pullback call. You also left two men below.”

“Yes.”

“And if they ask whether you could have gone deeper?”

“We could have,” Rowan said. “We might not have come back.”

Sena barked a humorless laugh. “There’s your problem. You still leave room for pride.”

She stepped closer, voice low. “If you dress this up, they’ll smell it. Say what happened plain. Say you chose the living in front of you over the chance of bodies farther in. Some will hate you anyway. Better that than letting Pike’s pets tell the story first.”

Then she jabbed a finger into his chest. “And don’t put my name where I can’t see it. I’ll sign in person or not at all.”

“Hall board at second bell,” she added. “Don’t be late. They lock the door once seated.”

“You’re coming?”

“If I can stand. Someone should watch them lie.” She started away, then glanced back. “And if Benn’s sister is there first, let her swing once. You’ll have earned it.”

They got no farther than the medic tents before a junior orderly blocked them and said Jeren was sleeping under tincture and not fit for visitors. Rowan argued. Lio tried charm. Both failed.

So they turned toward the lower quarter.

By the time they reached Rowan’s street, the sun had climbed high enough to wake the stink of drains and old ash. His father’s door was shut. Good. Or bad.

A woman stood three houses down speaking to old Mistress Corl. Benn’s sister, Rowan realized as she turned and saw him.

Her spine stiffened. Mistress Corl followed her gaze and gripped the doorframe hard.

“Rowan Vale,” Benn’s sister said. “You came back.”

“I did.”

“My brother didn’t.”

Lio shifted half a step behind Rowan and left him the line.

Rowan said, “No.”

She came down the step. “Then tell me why I had to hear from yard mouths that you led men out while Benn stayed under.”

The street went very quiet.

“Because the collapse cut us,” Rowan said. “Because we found another path with injured people who wouldn’t survive waiting. Because when the choice came, I took those I could still bring out.”

“You left him.”

“Yes.”

The word landed harder than any defense.

Her eyes shone. “Did he call for help?”

Rowan remembered dust, dark, failing stone, and the bell from somewhere too deep. Not Benn’s voice. Not Derren’s.

“No,” he said. “Not where I was.”

“Then you don’t know he’s dead.”

“No.”

Mistress Corl whispered, “But you think it.”

Rowan met her gaze. “I think the lower workings turned worse after we withdrew.”

No lie. No comfort he couldn’t stand behind.

Benn’s sister drew a shaking breath. “If there’s a board, I’ll be there.”

“For what?” Lio asked carefully.

“To hear whether the guild intends to search,” she said. “And to hear whether this town means to count my brother as lost because it’s cheaper.”

That startled Rowan enough to show. She saw it.

“You’re not the only one who can smell paper games, Vale.”

From behind them came a knock of wood on stone.

Elias Vale stood at the mouth of the lane in his work coat over yesterday’s shirt, face drawn from too little sleep and too much waiting. He held a split kindling stave he’d evidently forgotten he was carrying.

He looked first at Rowan to make sure he was standing. Then at Benn’s kin. Then at Lio. He understood enough.

“Inside,” Elias said.

Not loud. Worse than loud.

Rowan inhaled once, smelling warm brick, drain water, and onion grease on his fingers.

Second bell this afternoon. A board. Witnesses. Missing men. A fee he could not pay. And now his father with fear already wearing anger.

Rowan stepped toward the door.

Behind him, Benn’s sister said, “If they bury this, I will not keep quiet.”

Neither would he.

He went inside anyway.
