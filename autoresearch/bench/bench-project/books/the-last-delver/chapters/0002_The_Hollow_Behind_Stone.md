# Chapter 2: The Hollow Behind Stone

The shardmice came back in waves.

Not a clean rush. Not courage. Hunger and pressure and the kind of blind persistence that made Rowan hate them more than larger things with teeth. He could hear them before he saw the glimmer of their backs in the dark—tiny claws scratching over broken stone, little bodies pressing through rubble, a rustling tide that never quite stopped.

“Don’t stamp,” Rowan said.

His own voice sounded thin in the crushed pocket of tunnel. Dust still hung in the air; every breath scraped. Beside him, Lio was trying not to cough and failing at it in short, sharp bursts. One of the older workers—Mett, broad-shouldered, bleeding from the scalp—had found a fist-sized rock and was gripping it as if that would settle matters.

“That’s your instruction?” Mett muttered. “Not ‘run’?”

“If we run blind, we die tired.”

A pause.

Then, from the dark, a woman’s frayed voice. “That was almost funny.”

Toma.

Relief hit Rowan so fast it made his knees weak for half a second. He covered it by crouching near the rubble choke where the passage widened into the split chamber. He still couldn’t see much. Their lamp was gone, smashed in the collapse, and all they had now was the dull, intermittent glow leaking from fungus crusted in a crack overhead—weak green smears that turned faces into masks.

“You’re alive,” Rowan said.

“Disappointed?” Toma asked. Her humor dragged against pain.

“Give me a minute and I’ll decide.”

That got a breath of a laugh from Lio. Good. If they could still laugh, they could still move.

Rowan edged closer to the sound of the mice. He could smell them now under the grit and wet stone: a sour mineral stink, like old copper soaked in spoiled broth. Shardmice fed on vein leavings, fungus growth, scraps of dead things. Enough of them could strip a body to ribbons. Not quickly. That was the trouble.

“How many?” Toma asked.

“Too many to stand here.” Rowan lowered himself and pressed one palm to the floor.

He had no clean way to explain what happened next. He never did. It wasn’t hearing, not exactly. Not touch. The stone carried shape in strange, ugly ways. Pressure. Hollows. Stress lines. He felt it sometimes through his boots, sometimes through a wall, sometimes in his jaw when a tunnel wanted to come down. Since he was little, he’d known when the cellar under the Vale house needed shoring before his father noticed the shift. He had learned, quickly, not to talk about it much. People liked useful oddities only until they became unnatural ones.

Now the ground beneath his hand seemed to breathe.

Not air. Space.

He frowned and leaned closer, ignoring the grit biting into his palm. Behind the scrape of mice and the ragged breathing of the others, the stone answered in a dull inward hush. To the left, packed collapse, dense and final. Ahead, the old passage narrowing. To the right—

A cavity.

Not open tunnel, maybe. Something behind stone. Thin barrier. Old work or a natural pocket. Hard to tell.

Rowan looked up toward the right-hand wall. In the weak glow it looked like the rest of the chamber: rough gray-brown rock, old pick scarring, a wash of dust from the cave-in. Nothing obvious.

Lio saw his attention shift. “What?”

“Maybe not a dead end.”

Mett snorted. “You can see through rock now?”

“No. I can hear what your better ideas sound like, though, and it’s very quiet.”

“Boy—”

“Enough,” Toma said, sharper than Rowan expected.

She emerged from the dark in stages: one hand against the wall, one shoulder slumped, hair ghost-pale under dust. Her left trouser leg was torn and stiff with blood down the calf. She had Benn’s short-bladed salvage knife in one hand, which answered one question and raised three worse ones.

Rowan’s eyes caught on it. “Where’s Benn?”

Her jaw tightened. “Lost him at the lower bridge when the line snapped. Derren shoved me clear when the first support went. I haven’t seen either since.”

The words landed heavy. Lio swore softly.

Survival-determine-toma-fate-and. Not a phrase Rowan would ever have used, but the meaning sat there all the same: Derren Holt, Benn, maybe others, somewhere below or dead or trapped between. Nothing he could do about it yet. That burned.

The scratching grew louder.

Toma followed his gaze to the right-hand wall. “You think there’s space there?”

“I think there’s something. Thin stone maybe. Or old fill over a worked opening.”

“And you know this how?”

Rowan almost said because the wall sounds wrong. He bit it back. Bad enough being the fool who’d led them into a rescue run below a Pike-controlled shaft. Worse if he started talking like a tunnel spirit had whispered in his ear.

“I noticed draft changes before the collapse,” he lied, or half-lied. “And the stone here isn’t carrying the same as the left side.”

Mett gave him a look. “That sentence means nothing.”

“It means if you want to stay and count mice, do that.”

Resistance flickered across Mett’s face, but another wave of scratching helped Rowan’s argument better than words could. Little gleams shifted in the gap between fallen slabs. A mouse squeezed halfway through, crystalline growths glittering along its spine. Lio smashed it with a stone before it got fully in. The crunch turned Rowan’s stomach.

“Right,” Toma said. “Say there’s a hollow. Can we get through it?”

“Depends what’s behind it.”

“Comforting.”

“It’s been a hard night for comfort.”

He rose and tested the wall with the pry bar. Not striking. Listening. The first touch rang dull. The second, lower down, gave back a flatter note. He moved a handspan to the right and tapped again.

There.

Thin. Not by much, but enough.

“Help me clear this section,” Rowan said.

Mett hesitated. “If that wall’s load-bearing—”

“So is this chamber, and the mice don’t care. Choose.”

That finally moved them. Lio, shaky but game. Toma, limping and hard-eyed. Mett and the fourth survivor, a narrow-faced woman Rowan barely knew as Sena, both exhausted and filthy and too scared not to help. They worked with hands and broken stone and the pry bar, easing loose rubble away from the suspicious section while trying not to shake the chamber apart.

The labor took longer than Rowan wanted. His shoulders burned. Dust got into his mouth until his tongue felt furred. Twice he froze them when the stone gave a warning whisper through his fingertips—a tension shift, a tiny settling groan deep in the wall. Twice they waited while the tunnel chose not to fall.

“Nothing,” Mett said at last, breath rough. “This is madness.”

“No,” Rowan said, touching the exposed seam. “This is patched.”

He could feel it now. Not natural rock. Mortar long dried. Old blockwork hidden under a skin of cave dust and collapse grime. Whoever had sealed it had done decent work, but not meant to fool anyone forever. Just long enough.

Toma crouched beside him and ran her fingers over the line. “By the bells.”

“Don’t say that down here,” Lio muttered.

Nobody laughed at that one.

Rowan wedged the pry bar into the seam and leaned. Pain flared in his bruised side. The bar slipped with a screech.

“Again,” Toma said.

He reset. Mett joined him, muttering all the while. Together they put weight into it. The stone patch shifted a finger’s width inward and a stale breath of air sighed out over them.

Cooler than the tunnel. Dry. Old.

Every head lifted at once.

Lio grinned with all the recklessness of fourteen years and no sense. “That’s a hole.”

“That’s air,” Sena whispered, and sounded close to crying.

The mice came harder then, spurred by movement or scent. Three forced through the rubble gap together. Mett crushed one under his rock; Toma stabbed another and cursed when it twisted before dying; the third launched at Lio’s ankle and Rowan batted it away with the pry bar, sending it skittering against the wall with a sound like thrown crockery.

“No more testing,” Rowan snapped. “Move.”

They hauled on the false wall until one block tore free and slammed down, raising a burst of bitter dust. Behind it lay blackness and a slit of passage no wider than Rowan’s shoulders.

It also smelled different.

Old mineral dryness, yes, but underneath it something sharper, cleaner. A trace of moving air. And, faintly, the electric tang that clung to worked relic-metal.

Cities ran on what places like this gave up. Ore for foundries. Monster hide and bone for tools and trade. Fungus vats. Salvage from old machine rooms sealed since prior ages. Purified cores if a crew got very lucky and didn’t die for it. That was why guilds owned maps, licenses, entry points, and every legal scrap dragged to the surface. A hidden bypass near a productive shaft wasn’t just a route. It was profit. Control. Claims.

Rowan stared into the dark slit and thought, absurdly, of clerks with inked fingers arguing percentages over bodies not yet cold.

“Can you fit?” Toma asked.

“Yes.”

“Can the rest of us?”

“Only one way to learn.”

Mett looked at the narrow opening, then at the active rubble gap where shardmice kept testing. “I hate your plans.”

“I hate this mine. We all compromise.”

Toma almost smiled. “I’ll go after you.”

“No. Lio goes second. If the passage narrows, he’s the smallest.”

Lio puffed up. “Not by much.”

“By enough. Then Sena. Then Toma. Mett last.”

Mett bristled. “You ordering me now?”

“Yes.”

“Why am I rear?”

“Because if the mice break through while we’re halfway in, I’d rather the biggest idiot had the rock.”

Toma barked a laugh. Mett glared, then grunted. “Fine.”

Rowan slid into the opening feet first, turning one shoulder, then the other. Stone scraped his ribs. Loose grit ran cold down the back of his shirt. For three claustrophobic breaths the passage clamped around him so tightly he thought they had traded one kind of death for another.

Then it widened enough to crawl.

“Come on,” he called softly.

Lio’s boots rasped behind him. The sound of the others followed in uneven sequence. Outside, the mice intensified into frantic scratching and tiny impacts. One squeezed far enough through the first gap to shriek, a glassy, awful sound.

Rowan kept moving.

The bypass dipped. Old masonry gave way beneath his hands—squared stones, then natural shelf, then more worked support where some forgotten crew had once stabilized the route. He stopped and pressed his palm to the floor.

The passage carried empty space below and ahead. Good. But not all of it was sound. To the left, a shear crack ran parallel to their route. Thin now. Waiting.

“We need to go one at a time over the next stretch,” Rowan said.

From behind him, Mett’s muffled curse: “You just said we were crawling together.”

“I was wrong. Try to enjoy the novelty.”

Toma’s voice came low from farther back. “What changed?”

“Floor’s bad.”

“How bad?”

He listened again, jaw clenched. The answer came through the stone in ugly little clicks. “Bad enough that if Mett charges over it like an ox, I’ll know what his boots taste like from below.”

“Rude,” Mett said.

“Useful,” Toma countered. “Spacing?”

“Ten breaths between each of us. Light hands. Keep weight close. No sudden shifts.”

Sena started to say something and lost it to a cough. Rowan heard panic opening in that sound and spoke before it could spread.

“You only need to do what I tell you for twenty steps.”

“Twenty?” Lio said. “That’s all?”

“If I say eighty, will that help?”

“No.”

“Then twenty.”

He moved first, slow and low. The air cooled further, brushing his face from ahead. Good sign. The floor under him changed texture after six paces—less solid, more hollow-backed. At twelve, a faint crack answered his weight. At fifteen, his right hand found the wall opening into broader dark.

He exhaled. “Off the weak run. Lio, come.”

Lio began counting breaths loudly, lost track at four, swore, then came anyway. The floor clicked but held. Sena made it with a choked sob. Toma crossed in controlled silence, though Rowan heard how hard she was biting down on pain.

Mett started too fast.

“Slow,” Rowan hissed.

“I am slow.”

The floor answered with a brittle pop.

Everybody froze.

Even the mice behind them seemed to vanish for one long, terrible second.

Then rubble pattered somewhere below the weak run, not a collapse, just a warning. Mett breathed through his nose. Once. Twice. Finished the crossing with care.

When he rolled beside them in the wider dark, Rowan let himself sit back against the wall. His muscles trembled with delayed strain.

“What is this place?” Sena whispered.

Rowan looked around. The bypass had opened into a low service corridor, half natural, half shaped by tools older than South Third’s current workings. The fungus here glowed in thinner smears, enough to sketch outlines: bracket mounts on the wall where lamps had once hung, rust-fused rails for a cart too small for modern ore bins, a line of old sigils chiseled into one lintel and partly scoured away. Dust lay thick and undisturbed except where trickles of collapse grit had spilled in from above.

“Something abandoned,” Toma said.

“Which means unsafe,” Mett added.

“Everything below guild depth is unsafe,” Rowan said. “Difference is whether anyone bothered charging us for the map.”

Toma gave him a sidelong glance. “Your father talk like that too?”

“My father says it with less charm.”

That almost settled them. Almost.

Then, faintly, from deeper in the corridor, came a human sound.

Not words. A groan.

All five of them went still.

Lio whispered, “You heard that too?”

“Yes.”

The second sound was clearer: someone dragging breath through pain.

Benn? Derren? Another trapped worker? Rowan was already pushing upright before the thought finished. Toma caught his sleeve.

“Wait. Could be a lure.”

“For shardmice?” Mett said. “They don’t imitate people.”

“No,” Toma said, eyes on the dark ahead. “Other things do.”

Fair point. Rowan tasted metal at the back of his throat. This lower space smelled older than the current workings, and old routes held old problems. But the groan came again, weak and very human.

“We check,” he said.

“Of course we do,” Toma replied, and let go.

They moved carefully along the corridor, Rowan in front with the pry bar, Toma limping close enough to intercept if something lunged from the side. The stone underfoot remained mostly sound, though he felt dead pockets in the walls and one sealed chamber beyond a curve. The air tasted of rust and stale water.

The groans led them to a broken junction where a partial collapse had dropped masonry across a side room. A man lay pinned from the thigh down under a slab and a twisted length of old rail. Not Benn. Not Derren. Younger than either, face gray with pain, jacket marked with one of the lower-contract crews Rowan had seen at the shaft mouth.

The man flinched at their approach, raising a shard of metal like a knife. “Back off.”

“If we wanted you dead, we’d have taken longer,” Rowan said.

The man blinked, trying to focus. “You from surface?”

“No.”

“Guild?”

That one made Rowan laugh once without humor. “Do I look licensed?”

“Hard to tell in the dark.”

Toma crouched to inspect the pinning slab. “Name.”

“Jeren.”

“How many with you?”

“Three. Collapse took two. One ran.” His eyes shifted around them. “You got water?”

Sena handed over the skin before anyone else moved. Independent motive, Rowan thought dimly; not kindness alone, but the simple fact that another living body meant better odds if worse things found them. Good. Smart. He respected smart.

Jeren drank, coughed, passed it back with shaking fingers. “Heard mice. Thought that was it.”

“Maybe still is if we keep chatting,” Mett said.

Rowan knelt by the slab and set his hand to stone.

This was different from the false wall. Cleaner. A weight problem. Levering might free the leg or crush it flat. He listened through the contact. Old rail under tension. Slab balanced ugly. One wrong lift and the room would shed half its broken ceiling.

“We can do this,” he said slowly, “but not cleanly.”

Jeren’s lips peeled back from his teeth. “Meaning?”

“Meaning your leg’s likely broken already. We move the weight wrong, it gets worse. We leave you, the mice decide later.”

Jeren swallowed. “Fine choice.”

“Best one available.”

Toma looked at Rowan, measuring him. “You have a plan?”

“A bad one, which puts it ahead of the alternatives.”

Mett groaned. “I knew this was coming.”

Rowan pointed. “You and me take the slab. Toma stabilizes the rail. Lio, when I say, drag Jeren by the shoulders. Sena keeps watch behind us.”

“Why me?” Lio asked.

“Because if the slab shifts onto my hands, I’d like someone else in charge of the pulling.”

“That’s not better.”

“Nothing tonight is better.”

They set themselves. Stone bit Rowan’s palms. His bruised side throbbed like a pulse under the ribs. He counted them in a low voice, then heaved with Mett while Toma jammed the pry bar beneath the twisted rail to keep it from snapping down.

The slab moved.

Not much. Enough.

“Now!”

Lio and Sena hauled Jeren backward. The trapped man screamed, a raw animal sound that filled the corridor. Something in the ceiling cracked. Rowan thought, for one sick instant, they had misjudged and would die under this side room after all.

Then Jeren came free in a rush, one boot tearing off under the slab. Toma threw herself clear as the rail slammed down. Dust burst out in a choking cloud. Pebbles rattled over Rowan’s shoulders.

But the room held.

Barely.

Jeren lay on his back sobbing through clenched teeth. His lower leg bent wrong beneath the torn trouser. Lio looked as green as fungus light.

“Alive,” Mett said, astonished.

“Don’t sound so offended,” Rowan replied, though he was breathing too hard to put much sting into it.

And then the world went still.

Not really still. People still panting. Dust settling. Far-off mice scratching. But layered beneath all of it, like a pane of cold glass sliding into place over his thoughts, came something else.

A sensation of being measured.

Rowan stiffened.

Across his vision, in the dark where there should have been nothing, pale blue lines assembled with clinical precision.

NOTICE: SUBJECT OBSERVED UNDER CLAIM-RELEVANT HAZARD CONDITIONS.  
PROVISIONAL RECORD CREATED.  

He did not move. He barely breathed.

The lines sharpened.

Unlicensed individual Rowan Vale has performed actionable route identification and live-salvage extraction within an active delving zone.  
Criteria for informal recognition met.  
Further evaluation pending survival.

There was no warmth in the words. No praise. No mystery-song. Just a clerk’s knife made of light.

Below it, one final line appeared.

Trait resonance detected: Residual Hearing (ungraded).  
Use acknowledged. Limits unknown.

Then the blue faded.

“Rowan?” Toma said.

He realized everyone was staring at him. Not because they saw the text—of course they hadn’t—but because he had gone rigid as a pinned body.

“What?” he said.

“You looked like you saw a ghost,” Lio whispered.

“Worse,” Rowan said, voice dry.

He looked down at his hand still on the stone floor, then into the old corridor ahead where colder air moved from a darkness none of them had mapped, and where Derren or Benn might still be alive.

Behind them, distant but unmistakable, came another sound through the depths.

Not the mice.

A bell, ringing once in the dark below.
