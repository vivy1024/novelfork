# Chapter 3: Paper Shields

The old service corridor did not feel abandoned anymore.

It felt watchful.

Rowan stood with one hand against the cold masonry and listened past the ragged breathing of the others, past Jeren’s muffled groans, past the drip of water from some unseen crack overhead. The air carried dust, stale damp, and that faint metallic tang he had noticed before—worked metal, not raw ore. Rails rusted into the floor ran ahead into the dark. Old sigils, worn almost blank, marked the wall every dozen steps.

Not decoration. Utility.

Something down here had once mattered enough to build properly.

“Are we moving or praying?” Mett asked. “Because I’d prefer the first.”

Lio shot him a look. “You can walk ahead, then.”

“No, thank you.”

Toma sat with his back to the wall, one arm around his ribs. “If we stay, Jeren stiffens up. If we move too fast, he drops. Pick a misery.”

Sena crouched beside Jeren, checking the cloth around his leg. “He can move if we help him. Not run.”

Jeren gave a pained laugh that ended in a hiss. “Wasn’t planning on it.”

Rowan pushed away from the wall. “We go back.”

Mett stared. “Back? Through the mice?”

“Not through the pocket we came from.” Rowan pointed with the pry bar toward the draft. “This was service access. Service access leads somewhere people used. Better odds than waiting here.”

“And Benn? Derren?” Lio asked.

Rowan looked down the dark beyond the rails. No bell now. No voices. Just buried hush.

“If I hear them, we turn. If I don’t, dragging six half-broken people deeper into unmapped ground gets more of us killed.”

Mett folded his arms. “Convenient.”

Rowan met his eyes. “You think pushing farther with Jeren’s leg and Toma’s ribs and three lamps between us is smart?”

Mett held his stare, then looked away. “I think leaving men behind gets remembered.”

“So does leading a chain of bodies into a hole.” Rowan kept his tone level. “Pick which story you want told.”

No one spoke.

They built the march ugly and practical. Toma and Lio took Jeren’s weight between them. Sena carried the spare lamp. Mett muttered but hauled rubble aside when the corridor narrowed. Rowan led with the bar in one hand and the stolen pulse of blue memory still needling at the back of his thoughts.

Residual Hearing.

Ungraded.

Pending survival.

The march back was slower than fear wanted and faster than pain liked. Their boots clicked on old rail, slipped in grit, splashed through runoff. The corridor widened once into a chamber lined with collapsed shelving and split ceramic jars. Fungal growth crawled over one wall in pale shelves, giving off a sour smell. Sena slowed.

“That’s cutter mold,” she said.

“Useful?” Rowan asked.

“If it’s live and clean, alchemists buy it. This patch isn’t. Too old.”

Her eyes tracked the masonry and floor, already doing sums.

Good.

Cities above survived because places below kept producing value: dungeon ore, salvage, relic-tech, alchemical fungus, monster materials, and purified cores. Everybody lived off the Delve one way or another. Which was why the guild’s hand stayed around every throat that needed it. Guilds controlled entry points, licensing, maps, and legal salvage rights.

If they said you didn’t exist, you didn’t get paid.

Rowan filed the chamber away and kept walking.

Twice he stopped and listened.

Once because of scratching behind a wall that turned out to be water shifting grit.

Once because he thought he caught iron striking iron, three beats apart.

He raised a hand. Everyone froze.

“What?” Lio whispered.

Rowan held his breath.

Nothing.

“Keep going,” he said, though the skin at the back of his neck stayed tight.

They found the exit by draft first, then by light. Not daylight. Lampglow seeped through a bent iron grille where the service passage climbed toward a narrow maintenance throat. Someone had bricked part of it over long ago, but the upper section had cracked with age. Fresh air slid through the gaps, carrying coal smoke, hot oil, and the distant human stink of too many bodies packed in one yard.

Surface, or near enough.

Mett nearly laughed. “I could kiss whoever built ugly.”

“Climb first,” Rowan said.

The grille did not want to yield. Rust flaked under the pry bar. Old mortar snapped in hard little reports. Rowan worked slower after that until there was a person-wide gap.

Lio peered through. “Storage cut. I know this place. Off the old sorting yard.”

“Anyone there?”

Lio listened, then shook his head. “Not in the cut itself.”

“Good. Out. Quiet.”

“Quiet?” Mett said. “We’re alive.”

“And if people above would rather not answer why we’re alive, I’d like half a minute before they start talking.”

Sena frowned. “You think they’d hide it?”

Rowan looked at her. “You think they won’t?”

They squeezed through one by one. The storage cut beyond smelled of stale rope, dust, and spilled lamp oil. Broken baskets leaned against the wall. A handcart lay on its side with one wheel gone. From beyond a warped timber door came the wash of voices, boots, and once, sharply, a foreman’s whistle.

Jeren sagged against the wall. “If this is concealment, I hate to see welcome.”

“Stay here,” Rowan said.

Mett barked a laugh. “Right. We’ll just crouch in a hole while you sort the law.”

“You want to go out first, be my guest.”

“I want not to be hidden like stolen tools.”

Lio cut in. “Then stop sounding like one.”

Mett stepped toward him. “Say that again.”

“Enough.” Rowan kept his voice low. “We go out together in one line. Injured first. Mett, keep that mouth for witness, not panic. Lio, don’t start what I’ll have to finish.”

Toma snorted painfully. “He says, like either of them listens.”

Sena looked between Rowan and the door. “What are we hiding?”

“The corridor.” Rowan felt all of them focus on him. “Not forever. From whoever decides we entered where we weren’t supposed to and therefore found nothing worth paying for.”

Mett’s expression sharpened. “So there is something worth paying for.”

“There might be. Old rails. Masonry. mold chamber. Worked metal smell. Maybe infrastructure, maybe salvage, maybe nothing. But if we walk out shouting about an unmapped old cut, someone richer than us owns it by sundown.”

“That’s theft.”

“That,” Rowan said, “is procedure with better boots.”

Jeren, white-faced against the wall, muttered, “He’s right.”

Sena exhaled. “I hate that he’s right.”

“So do I,” Rowan said. “Ready?”

They were not ready. They went anyway.

The door stuck. He put his shoulder into it and it opened with a groan. The sorting yard beyond was a smear of lantern light, stacked ore bins, timber braces, and people moving with the jerky urgency that came after a disaster had already happened and paperwork was beginning.

A line of guild functionaries stood near the shaft mouth with slates in hand. Two watchmen kept back clusters of workers and family. Pike Holdings’ green-and-black banner hung from a post over the main platform. Rowan’s stomach tightened.

Joren Pike himself was there, coat clean despite the dust, talking to a guild registrar with the calm patience of a man explaining losses he did not intend to carry.

“—unauthorized entry,” the registrar was saying. “No seal cleared. No assignment recorded. If men went below after closure, responsibility is limited.”

A woman in an ore-hauler’s apron shoved forward and had to be checked by a watchman. “My brother was on shift!”

“Madam,” the registrar said, not looking at her, “the active roster is under review.”

Then someone in the yard saw them.

A dropped basket hit the ground.

“They’ve got people,” a man whispered. Then louder: “Out the side cut!”

Everything changed at once.

Workers surged. Watchmen cursed. Questions hit from every angle. Hands reached. Names got shouted. Lio nearly dropped under the attention until Toma snapped at them to let the wounded breathe.

Rowan got Jeren settled onto an overturned crate just as a guild clerk in a gray coat arrived, red-faced and sweating.

“Names,” the clerk said at once. “State your names and assignment line.”

“No physician first?” Sena demanded.

“Names,” he repeated.

Rowan gave his. The others followed. When Jeren tried, the clerk cut in.

“No listed Jeren in South Third.”

“Because I was posted lower relay two days ago and your slate runner can’t keep up,” Jeren rasped.

The clerk’s pen stopped. “You were posted? By whom?”

“Foreman Kell.”

“Foreman Kell is unavailable.”

“Dead, then,” Jeren said. “Write that faster.”

Behind the clerk Joren Pike approached. He took in the survivors in one sweep, then rested his attention on Rowan.

“This is an unexpected relief,” Pike said.

His voice was smooth enough to skate on. Rowan had heard it once before, from a distance, when his father had come back from arguing debt terms and spent the rest of the night chopping kindling that did not need chopping.

“Is it?” Rowan asked.

Pike’s eyes flicked to the storage-cut door behind them. “The shaft roster did not place any of you in authorized rescue operations.”

“There wasn’t one,” Lio said.

Pike ignored him. “So the question becomes where, exactly, you emerged from.”

Rowan said nothing.

The registrar looked up from his slate. “That is a material question.”

Toma gave a harsh laugh that bent him double. “Funny. I thought the material question was the collapse.”

“We are investigating the collapse,” the registrar said.

“By burying it under words,” Sena snapped.

“Careful,” Pike said mildly. “Panic and rumor harm everyone in a district already under strain.”

There it was.

Rowan wiped grime from his mouth. “Men were trapped. We went in. More trapped after the second fall. We found a way out.”

“A way out,” Pike repeated. “Describe it.”

“No,” Rowan said.

The yard seemed to pause around that one syllable.

The clerk blinked. “You do understand that all subterranean routes, active or defunct, fall under guild survey authority?”

“I understand that if I describe an unmapped route before witness statements are taken, it stops being our rescue path and turns into someone else’s secured claim.”

The registrar drew himself up. “You are unlicensed.”

“Still alive enough to notice patterns.”

A few nearby workers made sounds that might have been laughs.

Pike studied Rowan more carefully. “And what would you gain by obstructing an inquiry?”

“Maybe less obstruction than you’d gain by narrowing one.”

One of the watchmen barked, “Mind your tongue.”

Rowan turned his head just enough to look at him. “Then mind your dead.”

Lio sucked in a breath. Sena shut her eyes briefly. Toma looked almost proud, which meant he was concussed.

Pike lifted a hand before the watchman stepped in. “Strong feelings are natural. But law is not improvised by boys with dust on their faces.”

“No,” Rowan said. “It’s written by men who own ink.”

The woman in the apron gave a sharp little laugh. Others took it up. Not many. Enough.

The registrar’s ears reddened. “If you have a claim to submit, it requires rank, sponsorship, and proper filing.”

There. The shape of the cage, spoken plainly.

Rowan felt something settle cold and clean inside him.

No rank, no claim.
No claim, no salvage.
No salvage, no money.
No money, no leverage.

And without leverage, Pike could lean on his father until the house, the debt, and every choice around them belonged to someone else.

The city lived on what came out of holes in the earth, and the guild controlled the mouths of those holes. Entry points. Licenses. Maps. Legal salvage rights. A man without papers might bleed for value, but he did not own what his blood found.

Sena touched Rowan’s arm, light and brief. “Say less,” she murmured.

The registrar cleared his throat. “Given the irregularity of this event, statements will be taken individually. Pending review, no recovered route or material may be discussed publicly. Any attempt to enter South Third without authorization will result in detention.”

Mett, behind Rowan, muttered, “Convenient again.”

Pike heard him. “Necessary.”

“For whom?” Jeren asked from the crate. “I got dragged out through a passage your maps didn’t have. Either your survey is bad or your records are.”

The registrar started, “That matter—”

“—should be examined under guild process,” Pike finished smoothly. “Which requires order.”

“Order,” Toma said, “looks a lot like silence from down here.”

Pike’s expression did not change. “And noise looks a great deal like forfeiture.”

A pair of medics finally pushed through with stretchers and bitter-smelling tonic. They took one look at Jeren and shoved the officials aside without apology. For a minute there was only practical work—binding, lifting, checking pupils, asking who could walk.

In the churn, the gray clerk tried once more, lower this time. “If there is route information, surrendering it voluntarily may weigh in your favor.”

Rowan looked at the man’s slate, at the tidy ruled lines waiting to decide reality. “Will it weigh enough to make it mine?”

The clerk hesitated.

“No,” Rowan said.

The man made another mark. “Then you are refusing cooperation.”

“I’m refusing charity dressed as process.”

When the medics took Jeren and Toma toward the infirmary wagon, Lio moved to follow. Rowan caught his sleeve.

“Later,” Rowan said.

Lio pulled free. “He saved me.”

“And if you get penned as witness before you breathe, you won’t help him.”

Lio glared, then jerked his chin once.

A bell rang from the yard office. The sound shivered across Rowan’s nerves, too close to the deeper bell from below. For an instant the world narrowed. He felt the old corridor again, the hidden rails, the moving air behind dead stone.

Then blue light ghosted at the edge of his vision.

**Informal recognition conditions sustained beyond immediate hazard.**  
**Provisional record retained.**  
**Claim relevance noted.**  
**Legal standing insufficient.**

Rowan did not flinch, but it took effort.

Lio was saying something. “Rowan. Rowan.”

“I heard.”

“You looked gone.”

“Not gone.”

“Then listen now,” Lio said. “My aunt works wash at the provisional hall. They run qualification screens every fifth day for labor transfers and would-be delvers. Written basics, hazard calls, route law, tool handling. If you pass the first cut, they give you a probation token. You want papers? That’s the first door.”

Rowan stared at him.

Sena, overhearing, turned from the infirmary wagon. “That’s real?”

Lio nodded. “For pit-rats, haulers, runners. Anyone stupid enough to want below-work legal.”

“Or desperate enough,” Mett said.

“Same pool,” Lio replied.

The words landed heavier than the blue notice had.

First door.

Not a favor. Not a miracle. A process.

Pike was still across the yard speaking softly with the registrar, no doubt trimming names into something tidy enough to file. Rowan looked from the banner over the shaft to the infirmary wagon, to the storage-cut door hidden half in shadow.

Legal standing insufficient.

For now.

He could almost see the shape of what came next. Get rank. Even the lowest. Enough to file a salvage notice. Enough to put the old corridor on paper before Pike Holdings swallowed it whole. Enough to make his father’s debt negotiable instead of terminal. Enough to stop being easy.

“What’s required?” Rowan asked.

Lio gave a tired shrug. “Fee. Sponsor or bond note if you don’t have work record. Pass the screen. Survive long enough after that for the guild to stop calling it a clerical error.”

Sena let out a short breath that might have been a laugh. “Charming.”

Mett rubbed a hand over his face. “Fee kills it for half the district.”

Rowan’s eyes drifted back to the storage cut.

Old rails. Worked-metal tang. A mold room worth checking. Maybe more.

Not theirs yet. Not by law.

But the moment he said nothing to the wrong man and the right thing to the right slate, it could become a claim instead of a rumor.

Unless Pike got there first.

Across the yard, Joren Pike looked up and met Rowan’s gaze from thirty paces away.

Then Pike smiled.

It was small. Polite. Worse than anger.

The infirmary wagon rolled out. The registrar called for the next witness. The yard swallowed names, dust, and lies in equal measure.

Rowan watched Pike’s banner stir in the smoky night air and understood with unpleasant clarity that getting out of the hole had only carried him to the mouth of another one.

This one kept records.

And if he wanted to protect his father, hold Brackenside together for one more season, and keep the old corridor from vanishing into a rich man’s ledger, then surviving below would not be enough.

He would need a legal name under the guild seal.

He would need to qualify.

And somehow, before Pike moved, he would need the fee.
