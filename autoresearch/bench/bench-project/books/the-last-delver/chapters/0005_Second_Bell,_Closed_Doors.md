# Chapter 5: Second Bell, Closed Doors

Elias Vale did not wait for Rowan to sit.

The door shut hard enough to rattle the crockery. Their rooms smelled of lime dust, boiled onions, and the bitter salve on Rowan’s shoulder. Lane noise leaked through the shutters—cart wheels, a shout over weighed ore, a dog barking twice.

“You will not speak in that hall like you spoke in the yard,” Elias said.

Rowan kept one hand on the chair back. “Specific enough to obey, or do you want to try again?”

His father’s jaw flexed. Elias was not a large man, but years hauling cut stone had packed him hard through the shoulders. Today he looked older. Chalk sat in the lines of his palms. Fear sat in his eyes under anger laid on too fast.

“I want you alive,” Elias said. “I want this roof still ours next month. I want collectors not knocking because my son decided he’d make himself a righteous fool in front of men who can sign a paper and strip us bare.”

“So that’s it.” Rowan sat because standing made the room tilt. “Not whether Benn and Derren are still down there. Not whether Pike sat on a bad route and fed us into it. Just whether the paper comes for the walls.”

Elias slapped the table once. “Don’t use the missing boys as a knife on me. I know exactly who is still below.”

“Then act like it.”

“I am.” Elias dragged a hand over his face. “You think I haven’t seen this before? A collapse, a hearing, two men with better coats than sense deciding where blame lands. The hall doesn’t care who was brave in the dark. It cares who can be made neat on a ledger.”

Rowan said, “Then they’ll have to write small.”

For a heartbeat Elias looked as if he might laugh. He did not. He sat opposite Rowan. “If they ask what you found, say enough to show why you brought people out. No more. If Pike asks how far the old cut runs, you don’t know. If the board asks whether it opens into reclaimable workings, you say you’re not a surveyor. If they ask whether you withheld route detail, you say you were guiding injured people and trying not to die.”

“That part’s true.”

“Yes. Use truth. Short truth. Not the whole of it.”

Rowan studied him. “You’re scared they’ll take the route.”

“I’m scared they’ll take it,” Elias said quietly. “Or take you with it. There’s a difference.”

That landed harder than Rowan expected.

Elias went on. “Men like Pike don’t need the tunnel itself. They need your mouth. If you sound uncertain, they call you a liar. If you sound certain, they call you a thief. If you sound angry, they call you unstable. So don’t give them your temper.”

“I hadn’t planned to.”

“That would be a first.”

Rowan snorted despite himself. “There he is.”

“I’m not saying bow,” Elias said. “I’m saying choose where to plant your heels.”

Rowan tapped the table. “Did someone speak to you?”

Elias hesitated.

“Who?”

“Hadrik’s clerk. Came by after dawn. Asked after your condition. Asked whether your family intended to cooperate.”

“Polite threat, then.”

“Polite warning.”

“Same coat. Different weather.”

Elias hardened again. “Talk like that in front of the board and they’ll decide you’re trouble before you finish your first sentence.”

“I am trouble,” Rowan said. “For them.”

“And they are hunger for us. Keep that in mind.”

A knock rattled the door.

Elias opened it. Sena stood outside with one sleeve rolled and her chin set like she was ready to argue with a wall. Lio waited behind her with a folded packet under one arm.

“Am I interrupting domestic peace?” Lio asked.

“Yes,” Rowan and Elias said together.

Sena stepped in. “Good. Means you’re both still breathing.”

Lio offered Rowan the packet. “Copy request filed. Not granted yet, but the act of asking annoyed the right clerk.”

“What copy?”

“Preliminary collapse note. Section marks, assigned party, declared hazards. If Pike amended anything after the shift opened, I want him sweating before supper.”

Elias frowned. “You went fishing in records before the hearing?”

Lio looked bland. “I am shocked by the accusation that I can read and move at the same time.”

Sena planted both hands on the table. “The better question is whether Rowan can stand through this without swaying like a drunk.”

“I can stand.”

“You did not answer the sway part.”

Rowan grimaced. “Thank you for the tender concern.”

“I’m practical. If you faint, Pike wins the room.”

Elias looked from one to the other. “You’re both going with him?”

“Yes,” Sena said.

“If the hall permits,” Lio said.

“It won’t help,” Elias said.

“Maybe not,” Lio replied. “But being alone will help even less.”

Rowan pushed himself up. Pain bit through his shoulder. “Then let’s stop talking and go hear what price they put on honesty.”

As he passed, Elias caught his sleeve. “If the board gives you a path to walk away from this clean, take it.”

Rowan held his gaze. “If clean means leaving Benn and Derren buried under paperwork, no.”

“I know,” Elias said, and let go.

They walked uphill through the market drift toward the hall district, where the stone got smoother and the drains less likely to choke. Carts heavy with ore had cut grooves into the street. A lift ledger hung outside one broker’s office behind dirty glass. Two fungus sellers argued over ration chalk by a corner post. Above them, guild stamps marked doors Rowan could not yet open. He smelled iron filings, lamp oil, hot yeast from a street baker, the city’s true fuels—hunger and money.

The hearing hall stood beside the registry annex. Gray stone, narrow windows, brass numerals over the arch blackened by soot and rain. Two guards at the entrance, pikes grounded, faces bored.

Inside, the air was cooler and smelled of dust, ink, and old wool. Benches lined the outer chamber. Petitioners filled half of them: a salvage widow clutching a cap, two cutters arguing in whispers, a man with a bandaged scalp asleep against the wall. Benn’s sister stood near the far side, shoulders rigid. She saw Rowan and looked away first.

Joren Pike did not.

He stood near the clerk’s dais in a coat too fine for a room meant to make workers feel small. He wore grief well, which made Rowan mistrust him more when Pike turned and arranged concern a breath too slowly.

“Vale,” Pike said, stepping over. “I’m glad you’re steady enough to attend.”

“Depends how you define steady.”

Pike’s gaze flicked to Rowan’s sling, then to Sena and Lio. “You’ve brought support.”

“I’ve brought witnesses,” Rowan said.

A tiny pause.

“Of course,” Pike said. “We all want clarity.”

Sena folded her arms. “Funny. You didn’t sound desperate for it in the yard.”

Pike gave her a courteous smile. “You were under strain, miss. I won’t hold your tone against you.”

“Good,” Sena said. “I’d hate to think tone mattered more than dead men.”

A clerk called names, and the room shifted.

Their hearing was in a side board room with six chairs, one long table, and windows too high to see out. At the head sat Mistress Corl from the ward, severe in dark blue; beside her, Registrar Heth with his neat beard and sharp eyes; and a third board seat filled by an older woman Rowan had not met, dry-faced, with callused hands and a delver’s old scar splitting one eyebrow.

The older woman studied him. “Sit if you need. Stand if you want. Fall over and I fine the ward.”

Corl sniffed. “You will do no such thing, Boardwoman Nare.”

“We’ll see. And before anyone starts pretending this room moves fast out of kindness—collapse boards can issue temporary orders same day when men are missing.”

Formalities came first. Names, shift designation, date of incident, recorded condition of recovered survivors. Then Heth folded his hands.

“This review concerns three matters. First, whether runner Rowan Vale’s emergency conduct warrants retention or withdrawal of provisional notice. Second, whether route information was unlawfully withheld from licensed recovery authority. Third, whether additional search obligation exists in South Third under current hazard conditions.”

Pike spoke first, as route contractor of record. Collapse signs had been limited. Crews had been warned. In the aftermath, confusion had been expected. He praised Rowan’s rescue effort while expressing concern that potentially critical location information had not been immediately surrendered, delaying formal response.

Delay. Rowan nearly laughed.

Instead he listened.

Heth turned to Rowan. “Your answer.”

Rowan kept Elias’s warning in mind. “We were trapped. The original path had failed behind us. I found a patched wall cut into older stone, opened it, and led survivors through a service corridor I had not known existed before that moment. We had injured people, unstable footing, poor air in sections, and no certainty the route remained passable. My priority was getting the living out.”

“Why not disclose full route details upon emergence?” Heth asked.

“Because I didn’t have full route details. I had a sequence of turns taken while hauling half-conscious people through dust and broken floor. I gave enough to confirm there was an alternate passage and enough to return if a proper team chose to risk it.”

Pike inclined his head. “Yet you refused my direct request for specifics.”

“Yes.”

“Why?”

“Because you were asking in the sorting yard before medics had finished with men who’d nearly died under your contract.”

Corl’s pen paused.

Pike said, “Emotion is understandable. Accuracy matters more.”

“That is accurate.”

Boardwoman Nare leaned forward. “Did you believe he meant to secure the route before the hall could mark it?”

“I believed he had interests that were not the same as mine,” Rowan said.

“And yours are?”

“Keeping the missing from becoming inconvenient.”

Nare grunted. “For the record, he is the first discoverer still breathing, the guide who brought survivors out, and the only coherent witness we have to the hidden seam.”

Behind him, Benn’s sister pulled in a sharp breath.

Heth cut through it. “Witnesses.”

Sena went first. She described the collapse, the bad air, Rowan finding the patched seam, Rowan refusing to leave Jeren pinned, Rowan guiding them through old workings none of them had mapped. When Heth asked whether Rowan had concealed information outside immediate necessity, she said, “I think he held back from Pike. I do not think he held back from the hall. Those are different things.”

Pike’s advocate, a thin man Rowan had barely noticed, seized on it. “So he did conceal.”

Sena looked him up and down. “If a dog snaps at your hand, and you move the meat behind your back until there’s a fence between you, is that concealment or common sense?”

Silence. Then Nare barked a laugh.

The advocate reddened. “This board is not interested in stable-yard wit.”

“No,” Sena said. “Just the kind that keeps people alive.”

Lio came next. He admitted he had not seen the deepest section himself. What he brought was process: transfer timing, intake confusion, statement collection in the yard before medics had stabilized survivors, missing-person acknowledgment delayed while route-right questions were asked. He never accused. He arranged facts in a row and let them point.

Pike’s advocate did not like that. “Your role is ward assistant, not hall officer.”

“Correct,” Lio said.

“So your opinions on procedure are irrelevant.”

“They would be if I had offered opinions. I offered times.”

Then Elias was called.

Rowan had not known whether his father would help or cut his knees out from under him. Elias stepped into the witness place with his cap in both hands.

“State your relation,” Heth said.

“Father.”

“And your testimony concerns?”

“Character. Condition after retrieval. And what he told me before second bell.”

Pike’s advocate leaned in.

Elias cleared his throat. “My son talks too much when he’s tired and too little when he’s hurt. Today he is both. But he is not a liar. He does not invent ground beneath his feet. If he says the old passage is dangerous, it is dangerous. If he says there may still be men below, then nobody should be closing a file to save ink.”

Rowan stared at him.

Elias kept going. “He also knows the worth of a thing. That makes him stubborn. So yes, he held his tongue from men he didn’t trust. I would have done the same.”

The room went very quiet.

Pike’s advocate rose carefully. “Are you aware your son’s refusal to disclose full route detail may have delayed organized search?”

Elias turned his cap once. “I’m aware organized search had not begun while men were still being sorted by which signatures they came attached to.”

Corl’s brows rose. Heth wrote something. Pike’s face did not move.

The pushback came harder after that.

Pike requested restricted disclosure for safety survey. Heth asked Rowan to provide turn counts, landmarks, air conditions, and barrier features for sealed record. Corl wanted hazard detail enough for a rescue team. Nare wanted to know why a patched wall existed in the first place and who had ever worked that older cut.

There it was—the edge of what lay beyond the seam.

Rowan chose each answer with care.

He described the patched seam. The stale, dry air beyond it. The old service corridor, the feel of older workmanship under newer collapse. He gave distances by strain and breath rather than exact measure. He noted the sloped floor, the cracked side niche, the place where the draft changed. Useful details. Not all details.

Nare caught it. “You are trimming.”

“Yes.”

“Why?”

“Because I don’t know what happens to a route once I say it in full in this room.”

Pike spread his hands. “It gets searched.”

“By whom?”

“Licensed recovery teams.”

“Contracted by whom?”

That finally put a blade in Pike’s tone. “By men capable of paying them.”

“Exactly.”

Corl intervened. “Enough. We can be honest about incentives without pretending they void procedure.”

Heth steepled his fingers. “The board can order a sealed technical deposition, accessible only to hall rescue authority pending hazard review.”

“Can the contractor see it?” Rowan asked.

“Not immediately.”

“Eventually?”

Heth did not answer quickly enough.

Rowan said, “Then no full deposition without missing-party status formally held open and witness note attached that I disputed contractor priority.”

Pike’s advocate started, “He cannot bargain with—”

Nare cut him off. “Anyone breathing can bargain. The question is whether we accept.”

The board conferred in low voices while everyone else sat still and pretended not to listen. Rowan heard little beyond chair creaks and his own rough breathing. His shoulder had gone from pain to numb heat.

When Heth looked up, his face had the blandness of a man about to alter someone’s life with dry sentences.

“Board ruling, preliminary. Provisional notice retained. Grounds: credible emergency leadership under collapse conditions, corroborated by witnesses. No immediate finding of unlawful withholding. Grounds: instability, injured survivors, and incomplete route certainty at point of first questioning.”

Rowan exhaled once.

“South Third missing-party status remains open for Benn Arl and Derren Holt pending three-day hazard review. Hall rescue authority will request sealed technical deposition from Rowan Vale under restricted access. Contractor may petition after review, not before.”

Pike’s jaw hardened by a degree.

“As to any emergency-service reduction of provisional filing burden,” Heth said, “that question is deferred. Rowan Vale may raise it after deposition and hazard review, if the record still supports it. No standing is granted on that point today.”

That took a different second to absorb.

Not a door open. Not even a hand on the latch. Just the sight of one further down the corridor.

Lio let out a slow breath. Elias closed his eyes briefly, as if thanking a god he didn’t trust.

Pike rose before dismissal. “Then I petition now for immediate contractor observer rights at the deposition.”

“Denied,” Nare said at once.

“On what basis?”

“On mine. Sit down.”

He did not. “Boardwoman, with respect, my liability—”

“Your liability,” Nare said, “is why you should be nowhere near a frightened unlicensed runner trying to decide how much truth he can afford.”

The hearing was dismissed in a scrape of chairs and low voices. Benn’s sister intercepted Rowan in the corridor.

Her eyes were red-rimmed, but her chin stayed lifted. “You kept them from closing it.”

“For now.”

“For now,” she echoed. “That matters.”

Rowan nodded. “I’ll give the hall enough to reach the place. I won’t hand it over blind.”

She studied him. “If they bring my brother up dead, I’ll still want someone to blame.”

“That’s fair.”

“If they don’t bring him up at all, I’ll want more than blame.”

“That’s fair too.”

She swallowed and stepped back.

In the outer chamber, Pike waited by the door as if he had every right to block the way. Petitioners flowed around him.

“Vale,” he said quietly. “You’ve made this harder than it needed to be.”

“For who?”

“For everyone. There’s money tied into South Third. Labor. Equipment. If rumor outruns order, we’ll have scavengers, false claims, panic among the crews.”

“Funny. I thought dead men might do that.”

Pike’s eyes flattened. “Do not mistake a temporary board mood for protection. You are still outside the fence.”

“And you’re still trying to count corpses before the search.”

Pike stepped closer, voice low enough that Elias and the others had to lean to catch it. “When the route is marked, you won’t be able to hold it with bare hands.”

Rowan met his gaze. “Wasn’t planning to ask nicely.”

That drew a disgusted breath from Elias and a short bark of laughter from Nare somewhere behind them.

Pike’s mask slipped for a blink—irritation, calculation, real hostility—then settled back into place, and he moved aside.

Outside, late afternoon carried the mineral tang of ore dust and distant rain. Rowan stood on the hall steps with his father on one side, Sena and Lio on the other, the district spread below in slate roofs and smoke.

“You won’t tell me that went well,” Elias said.

“No,” Rowan said. “I’ll tell you it went less badly than they wanted.”

Sena rolled her shoulders. “I’ve heard worse victories.”

Lio tapped the folded packet against his palm. “And now we use the board note before someone decides to lose it.”

Elias looked at Rowan, tired and angry and proud in ways he would never say plainly. “You’ve bought three days.”

“Then I’ll spend them.”

“On what?”

Rowan looked back toward the hall windows and thought of the patched seam, the dry draft beyond it, the old stone that had felt wrong under his fingertips.

“On deciding how much the hall gets,” he said. “And finding out who patched that wall before we did.”

Lio’s expression sharpened. Sena’s mouth went flat. Elias muttered something unkind under his breath.

From inside the hall, a runner burst through the doors carrying a sealed slate tube, nearly collided with a guard, and skidded to a halt when he spotted Registrar Heth hurrying after him.

“South Third update,” the runner said, breathless. “Fresh subsidence at the lower brace line. Search markers found disturbed.”

Everyone on the steps went still.

Heth’s gaze found Rowan first.

“Disturbed how?” Nare called from the doorway.

The runner swallowed. “From inside, Boardwoman.”
