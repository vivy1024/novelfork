# Chapter 7: Names Kept Off the Slip

Nessa did not look at Rowan when she said, “If you answer him here, answer small.”

The lower archive room smelled of glue, old dust, and lamp oil cooked too long. Somewhere upstairs a cart wheel squealed across stone, then thumped as if somebody had kicked it back into line. Rowan kept one hand on the edge of the table to steady his shoulder and turned only enough to see the doorway.

A man stood there in a dark review coat with the hall seal stitched at the cuff in silver thread gone gray at the edges. Older than Rowan had expected. Not soft, though. His face had the pinched look of someone who slept too little and blamed others for it. Behind him, the corridor lantern threw a hard bar of light across the floorboards.

Nessa closed the ledger in front of her with two fingers.

“Archivist Nessa Vale,” the man said. “You were asked to notify the desk when Rowan Vale arrived.”

“And I am notifying you now, Overseer.” Her voice stayed level. “Efficiently.”

The man’s gaze shifted to Rowan. “You have a habit of creating work.”

Rowan had met enough officials in the last three days to know which ones enjoyed fear and which ones merely used it. This one belonged to the second sort. More dangerous.

“I get told that a lot,” Rowan said.

Lio gave a soft snort from the shelf end. He had not left when the hall voice first came looking, and Rowan was suddenly glad of it. Lio looked unimpressed by authority on principle, which made him useful.

The overseer stepped into the room. His boots were clean. That alone said he had not been near the South Third cordon himself.

“You are Rowan Vale.”

“That’s been consistent so far.”

The man let the dry answer hang. “Overseer Talren. Hall review.”

He did not offer a hand. Rowan would not have taken it anyway.

Talren looked at the table. Maintenance abstracts. Binding slips. Route closures from years ago. The copied note from the deferred reinforcement order lay face down where Rowan had turned it. He hoped that looked casual. It had not felt casual.

“I’m told,” Talren said, “you requested access to restricted maintenance entries without a trade credential, survey seal, or sponsor.”

“Requested isn’t the same as received.”

Nessa made a tiny sound in her throat that might have been agreement and might have been a warning to stop talking.

Talren ignored her. “You were also present at South Third after closure. Then you came here. That sequence interests me.”

“Closure interested me first.”

“This is not the hearing chamber,” Talren said. “Do not fence.”

That landed closer than the words deserved. Rowan could still feel the boardroom in the back of his skull: polished rails, tired faces, Pike smiling like a man pricing lumber. He pushed it aside.

“You came to ask something,” Rowan said. “Ask.”

Talren studied him for a moment too long. “What were you looking for in the old maintenance records?”

Lio shifted his weight. Wood creaked. “That sounds broad.”

Talren’s eyes flicked to him. “And you are?”

“Not under review,” Lio said.

“Then you may stay quiet.”

“May,” Lio repeated pleasantly. “Useful word.”

Nessa coughed into her fist as if to hide a laugh. Talren did not appreciate being outnumbered.

Rowan answered before the man could start throwing people out. “I was checking whether the old route had prior instability notices.”

“That is not what you asked the desk runner for.”

So he had spoken to Sern already. Rowan felt the shape of the trap. Say too little, and Talren assumed concealment. Say too much, and the hall swallowed everything.

“I asked after closure history tied to the old service seam,” Rowan said. “Because I was in that section before collapse. Because two men are still missing. Because disturbed markers don’t happen on their own.”

Talren watched him without blinking. “You believe the disturbance came from below.”

“I believe something moved them.”

“Something.”

“There’s no rule saying facts have to become prettier when a hall coat hears them.”

Talren’s mouth flattened. Nessa busied herself with a stack of slips that did not need sorting. Lio looked delighted in the way of a man who did not have to testify later.

Talren said, “What else?”

There it was. Pushback, direct and clean.

Rowan could feel the folded copy in his pocket: transfer notation, partial index reference, enough to prove there had once been a nonpublic routing of materials related to the sealed conduit. Not enough to understand it. Enough to get taken from him if Talren decided the matter belonged wholly to review.

He made himself shrug. “Old patchwork. Deferred work that should’ve been done sooner. Same story as half the district.”

“That answer wastes my time.”

“That’s a risk every question takes.”

Talren stepped closer. Not a threat exactly. Pressure. The room seemed smaller with him in it.

“Listen carefully,” the overseer said. “The hall retained your notice pending deposition. That does not make you a technical witness. It does not grant you investigative privilege. If you are withholding records relevant to a live hazard review—”

“If he were withholding records,” Nessa cut in, still looking at her slips, “that would imply I let records walk out. Which I didn’t. So perhaps choose your verbs with care, Overseer.”

Talren turned his head. “Your desk has been loose this week.”

“My desk has been overrun this week,” Nessa said. “By contractors, runners, and review staff who discover the archive exists only when they want leverage.”

Lio grinned openly now. Rowan wished everyone would stop helping in ways that made things worse. He also wished they would never stop.

Talren exhaled through his nose. “Fine. Then I’ll state this plainly. Someone submitted a request this morning for nonpublic vault cross-index references tied to South Third civic works. That request carried a provisional claim interest notice.”

Pike. Maybe. Or someone working for him.

Rowan kept his face still.

Talren noticed the stillness. “You know what that means.”

“It means somebody wants old paper badly enough to dress greed as procedure.”

“Crude, but close.” Talren’s gaze sharpened. “Did you know of any vault reference before you came here?”

That was the dangerous question. Rowan tasted dust and old paste at the back of his throat. If he said no, and Talren already knew Nessa had fetched anything with index transfer notations, the lie would crack too fast. If he said yes, Talren would want source, wording, copies, all of it.

He bought time by adjusting the sling. Pain needled down his shoulder, useful because none of it had to be faked.

“I knew there was something missing from the seam record,” he said. “You don’t defer reinforcement on a sealed secondary line and then leave nothing but a closure note unless the real paper went elsewhere.”

Talren’s expression did not change. “That is an inference.”

“Yes.”

“Was it your inference alone?”

Nessa’s fingers stopped moving.

There it was again. Not only what Rowan knew. Who had helped him know it.

Lio spoke before Rowan could. “If you’re asking whether half the district can read a patch note and smell rot, then yes. Miracle of literacy.”

Talren’s eyes hardened. “I was not speaking to you.”

“No,” Lio said. “You were trying not to speak to her.”

The room went silent.

Rowan could almost hear the dust settling on the top shelf.

Talren looked at Nessa. “Leave us.”

Nessa raised her brows. “Absolutely not.”

“This is a hall matter.”

“This is an archive room. You can have it be one thing at a time.”

For a moment Rowan thought Talren might force it. Instead the overseer took one careful breath, the kind men used before deciding which rules were worth breaking in public.

“Very well,” he said. “Then all of you can hear. Vale, there are two active pressures on South Third now. One is the hazard review. The other is a private petition seeking authority to fund limited probe work if the district cannot meet costs. If that second paper clears, your route knowledge stops being your shield and starts being auction bait.”

Pike indeed.

Lio’s grin disappeared. “How fast?”

Talren glanced at him. “Fast enough that jokes are becoming charity work.”

That jolted the room more than shouting would have. Talren might be hostile, but he was not bluffing for theater.

Rowan said, “Who filed it?”

“A name you already suspect.”

“Then say it.”

“No.”

“Convenient.”

“Procedural,” Talren corrected. “And before you sneer, understand this: if Pike secures standing through district emergency funding, he can attach survey support and salvage lien options before your deposition ever opens. He does not need your exact route if he gets the area classified under hazard reclamation.”

The words landed cold.

That was the angle, then. Not stealing Rowan’s account in one piece. Smothering it inside a larger official seizure.

Nessa folded her hands. “You came here to warn him?”

Talren gave her a look. “I came here to determine whether he was feeding that petition with archive findings.”

“Why would he help the man who tried to strip his notice?” Lio asked.

“Debt. Fear. Inexperience. Pride.” Talren ticked them off like inventory. “People make alliances beneath themselves every day.”

Rowan almost laughed. Not because it was funny. Because it was true enough to sting.

“I’m not feeding Pike anything,” he said.

Talren watched him. “Then tell me what you found.”

“No.”

Lio looked sideways at him, quick and sharp. Nessa did not move at all.

Talren’s expression cooled by a degree. “You understand that refusal can be recorded.”

“Record it.”

“You are very young to sound this tired.”

“And you’re very calm for a man losing a race.”

That hit. Talren’s jaw shifted once.

Good, Rowan thought. If the man was running around after private petitions and hidden requests, then hall review was not in full control either. Which meant every player aboveground was scrambling over the same old bones.

Talren said, “What exactly do you think is at the bottom of this?”

Bottom. Wrong word. Rowan felt the memory of the wall under his palm, the odd mute answer to the bell-strike, not absence but swallowed sound.

“Not a store cellar,” he said. “Not a simple deadfall. There was older work behind the route wall.”

Lio looked at him sharply. Rowan had told him that much already, but hearing it spoken in front of Talren made it heavier.

Talren’s eyes narrowed. “Older than district works?”

“Yes.”

“You’re certain.”

“No,” Rowan said. “I’m stubborn. Different thing.”

Nessa let out a breath that might have been annoyance and might have been relief. Talren paced once beside the table, fingertips grazing the wood but never quite touching the papers.

“Describe it.”

“Why?”

“Because if this turns into a sealed-foundation issue, the hall has jurisdiction beyond guild closure review.”

That sounded too interested for Rowan’s comfort.

“And if I describe it,” Rowan said, “do I remain part of the answer? Or do I become the boy who made himself useful long enough to be put outside the door?”

Talren met his gaze. For the first time the man looked less irritated than honest.

“That depends on whether your information survives contact with other interests.”

A crooked answer. Maybe the best Rowan was going to get.

He gave Talren the safest truth he had. “Stone that didn’t match the tunnel. Cleaner joins. A section that should have rung hollow enough to tell me what sat beyond it, but the sound died wrong.”

“Dead resonance,” Nessa murmured before she could stop herself.

Talren’s head snapped toward her.

She closed her mouth.

There was no taking the words back.

Talren said softly, “So. That term was reached in this room.”

Nessa’s face turned unreadable. “That term exists in old civic notes. It does not belong to you because you heard it spoken.”

“No,” Talren said. “But now I know which shelf line to audit.”

Rowan swore under his breath.

Talren looked at him again. “Thank you. That helps.”

“It helps you.”

“It may also help keep your missing men from being written off as routine cave-in losses.”

“May,” Lio said. “Useful word.”

This time Talren almost smiled. It vanished at once. “If anything further occurs—new memory, new notes, any contact from Pike’s side—you report to review first.”

Rowan folded his arms, then immediately regretted what that did to his shoulder. “I report to the deposition order.”

Talren held his stare another heartbeat, then nodded once, as if placing him in a category he disliked but had expected to use.

“To the deposition, then,” he said. “Promptly, when called.”

He turned and left without another word.

The corridor swallowed the sound of his boots. Upstairs, a door banged and someone cursed about mold.

For three full breaths nobody spoke.

Then Nessa said, “Well. That could have gone worse.”

Lio barked a laugh. “That’s your bright reading?”

“He didn’t search Rowan. He didn’t freeze this room. And he told us Pike’s moved from slander to paperwork.” She finally looked at Rowan. “My darker reading is less cheerful.”

Rowan pulled the folded copy from his pocket and set it on the table.

Lio stared. “You had it on you.”

“Yes.”

“You told me not to carry stupid things in my coat.”

“You usually earn that warning.”

Nessa snatched the copy before either of them could touch it again. “Both of you, stop. If he had searched you, I would be writing a resignation in blood.” She unfolded the paper, eyes moving quickly. “I only let you copy the transfer notation because I thought the wording might help. I did not expect review to arrive before the ink dried.”

Rowan leaned against the shelf. It smelled of dry leather and mildew. “What does the index mark mean to you?”

“Not enough,” Nessa said. “The prefix is old civic vaulting, not guild holdings. But the suffix isn’t district standard. It may be a split reference. Or a later recatalog.”

Lio frowned. “In normal speech?”

“It means the paper we need was moved somewhere that wanted not to be found by ordinary clerks.”

“That’s much better normal speech.”

Nessa ignored him. “Talren will audit shelves tied to dead resonance now. He’ll do it through official channels, which means slower than panic but faster than comfort. If there’s a way to see where the cross-index lands, it has to be before he locks my access under supervisory sign.”

“Can you do that?” Rowan asked.

“Not directly. Main vault requests need upper countersign.” She tapped the page. “But old transfer bundles leave clerical scars. Routing slips. recopy errors. withdrawn binder gaps. Someone patient can follow scars.”

“You sound like you’ve done this before,” Lio said.

Nessa gave him a flat look. “I am an archivist. We call it Tuesday.”

Despite everything, Rowan’s mouth twitched.

Then footsteps rushed past the corridor, too fast for archive pace. A young runner appeared at the doorway, breathing hard.

“Nessa? Desk says you’re wanted topside. Review wants the South Third closure packet and the old civic maintenance line for—” He saw Rowan and stopped. “Oh. You’re him.”

“Tragic if true,” Rowan said.

The runner swallowed. “There’s more. District watch posted a second notice at the cordon. Fresh soundings.”

The room sharpened around that.

“What kind of soundings?” Rowan asked.

The runner looked between them, suddenly aware he had carried more than a summons. “No full report yet. But the watchman said the lower stone answered wrong. He said they struck near the broken marker line and got two returns. One close. One deep.”

No one moved.

Lio was first to find his voice. “Two echoes?”

The runner nodded. “That’s what he said.”

Rowan felt the old memory in his bones like a second pulse. The wall in the route. The muted answer. The sealed conduit. Not one hollow space, then. Or not one anymore.

Nessa folded the copied transfer note shut in her fist. “Go,” she told the runner. “Tell desk I’m coming.”

The boy fled.

Lio turned to Rowan. “You’re thinking of going to the cordon.”

“I’m thinking of twenty bad ideas.”

“Name a better one,” Lio said.

Nessa cut in hard. “A better one is not getting arrested at a sealed site while Pike tries to buy half the district around your feet.”

“He’s right to see it,” Lio said.

“He can see it from outside the ropes like every other fool with curiosity.”

Rowan pushed off the shelf. His shoulder ached. His head ached more. “If they’ve got a second return now, then whatever’s below isn’t staying still. Pell needs to hear it. And if Talren hears before us, he frames it before we do.”

Nessa stared at him for a long second. Then she opened her hand and looked at the copy once more.

“Take nothing written,” she said at last. “Nothing. If you’re stopped, you know nothing except that old repairs were neglected and the soundings changed.”

Lio folded his arms. “That sounds suspiciously like advice.”

“It is triage,” Nessa snapped. “There’s a difference.”

Rowan held out his hand for the paper.

She did not give it to him.

“No,” Nessa said. “You’ve proved you’ll carry a torch into powder if you think there’s a map on the other side. I’ll keep this. If I can chase the clerical scars before Talren drops shutters on the vault trail, I will.”

“And if somebody else is already in there?” Rowan asked.

Nessa’s mouth tightened. “Then I’ll learn what kind of shoes they wear.”

Lio blinked. “That’s unsettling.”

“It should be.”

For the first time since Talren entered, Rowan felt something close to steadiness. Not safety. Never that. But shape. Nessa would dig. Lio would complain and stay anyway. Pike was moving. Review was moving. South Third was answering wrong.

Everything ugly was becoming visible at once.

He nodded. “If you find the destination, get word to Pell, not the desk.”

Nessa pointed at him like a knife. “If you cross that cordon, I will deny knowing you in every archive on this side of the city.”

“You already do that most days.”

“This time I’ll mean it.”

Lio chuckled once, then sobered when Rowan started for the door. “We’re not crossing anything yet,” he said. “We’re going to listen first.”

“Listening has been expensive lately,” Rowan said.

“Yes,” Lio answered, falling in beside him. “And somehow you keep buying more.”

Behind them, Nessa called, “If either of you dies, I am not fixing the catalog entry.”

Rowan lifted a hand without looking back.

The archive corridor felt colder than before. Above, voices carried from the main room, clipped and urgent, paper sliding over wood, the whole building waking to the scent of trouble. By the time Rowan and Lio reached the stairs, a bell from the street side sounded once, flat and official.

Not alarm.

Notice.

The sort cities used when they wanted a crowd to gather while pretending they wanted order.

Lio paused at the landing and looked over the rail toward the entry hall. “That’s not for us.”

“No,” Rowan said, hearing the second, fainter vibration underneath it in his own memory. “That’s the problem.”

They went down fast.

Outside, evening air struck cool against Rowan’s face, carrying coal smoke, damp stone, and the metallic tang that always seemed to drift from the district when fresh earth was opened somewhere below. People were already turning toward South Third. Runners. Watch. A pair of contract men in neat coats moving too quickly for casual interest.

One of them wore Pike’s colors on the cuff.

Lio saw it too. “Well,” he said softly. “There’s your charity work gone.”

Rowan watched the men cut through the street toward the cordon.

Somewhere beneath the city, near broken markers and a wall that swallowed bell-sound wrong, stone had answered with two returns.

And now Pike was hurrying to hear them first.
